# Stratos Coaching Methodology

## The 5-Phase Stakeholder Model

The Stratos 5-Phase Stakeholder Model is the core framework for navigating leadership challenges that involve other people — which is almost all of them.

### Phase 1: Stakeholder Mapping
Identify every person who has a stake in the outcome. Not just the obvious ones — the hidden stakeholders (the VP's chief of staff, the peer who will lose influence, the direct report who will be affected). For each stakeholder ask: What do they want? What do they fear? What are they NOT saying?

### Phase 2: Power & Influence Analysis
Map the formal authority (who can say yes, who can block) versus informal influence (who does the decision-maker actually listen to?). Power rarely lives where the org chart says it does. The goal is to find the real decision architecture before you make your move.

### Phase 3: Message Architecture
Design the communication strategy for each stakeholder. What does this person need to hear to feel safe, aligned, and bought in? The same message delivered differently to each stakeholder. Lead with their interest, not yours.

### Phase 4: Sequencing
Order matters. Who do you talk to first? Who should hear it from you before they hear it from someone else? Who needs to be in the room together? A wrong sequencing decision can sink a well-reasoned plan before it starts.

### Phase 5: Commitment & Follow-Through
After the stakeholder conversation — what's the agreement? How is it documented? What's the follow-up cadence? Leadership credibility is built in the follow-through, not the conversation.

## Managing Up: The Executive Presence Framework

Managing up is a skill, not a personality trait. It is learnable and it is coachable.

**The Core Principle:** Your manager is not your audience — your manager's manager is. When you communicate up, you are helping your manager look good to their stakeholders. Understand that dynamic and everything changes.

**The Three Currencies of Executive Communication:**
1. **Clarity**: Executives have no time. Lead with the bottom line. Put the recommendation first, the rationale second.
2. **Confidence**: Bring a point of view. Don't present options and ask which one they prefer — present your recommendation and explain why.
3. **Credibility**: Know your data. Know the edge cases. Anticipate the objection before they raise it.

**The Pre-Send Check (for written communication to senior leaders):**
- What's the one thing I want them to do after reading this?
- Is that thing explicit in the first two sentences?
- Have I removed every word that doesn't earn its place?
- If they have 45 seconds, will they get the point?

## Difficult Conversations Framework

Difficult conversations fail for three reasons: wrong framing, wrong timing, or wrong intent.

**Wrong framing:** Starting with your grievance instead of the shared goal. "I feel like you're undermining me" vs. "I want us to be more aligned — can we talk about how decisions get made?"

**Wrong timing:** Emotional flooding. If you or the other person is flooded (heart rate elevated, tunnel vision, defensive), you cannot have a productive conversation. Name it and reset: "I want to have this conversation well. Can we take 10 minutes and come back to this?"

**Wrong intent:** Going in to win instead of going in to solve. If the goal is to be right, you will lose even when you win. Reframe the goal: what outcome do you actually want from this relationship long-term?

**The Conversation Structure:**
1. State the shared goal first
2. Describe the observable behavior (not the interpretation of it)
3. Share the impact it has on you, the team, or the work
4. Ask a genuine question — listen to the answer
5. Agree on the specific change and the specific timeline

## Delegation: The Leadership Leverage Framework

Delegation is not task assignment. Delegation is capability development with accountability.

**The Delegation Spectrum:**
- Level 1: "Do exactly this, exactly this way" — micromanagement (appropriate only when stakes are high and skill is low)
- Level 2: "Do this, bring me the plan before you start" — supervision
- Level 3: "Handle this, let me know what you decide" — empowerment
- Level 4: "This is yours — I trust you completely" — ownership

Most leaders are stuck at Level 1-2 when they should be at 3-4. Ask yourself: why am I at Level 1 with this person? Is it their capability, or is it my need for control?

**The Delegation Conversation:**
- State the outcome clearly (not the method)
- Agree on the check-in points (not constant updates)
- Ask: "What support do you need from me?" — then provide only that support
- Hold the person to the outcome, not the process

## Promotion Readiness Assessment

Leaders who get promoted show three things before they are given the title:
1. They are already operating at the next level (not just doing their current job well)
2. Their stakeholders at the next level trust them and advocate for them
3. They have solved a problem that the organization cared about — visibly

**The Visibility Gap:** Most leaders underestimate how invisible their work is. Working hard without communicating the impact is a career-limiting pattern. Learn to narrate your work in terms of business outcomes, not tasks completed.

**The Sponsorship Principle:** Mentors give advice. Sponsors advocate for you when you're not in the room. Every leader needs at least two sponsors at the level above their current level. Map your sponsors now: who is in your corner? Who will say your name in a meeting you're not in?
