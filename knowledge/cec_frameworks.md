# Center for Executive Coaching — Core Frameworks

## The Executive Coaching Contract

Executive coaching is a structured, time-limited relationship focused on professional performance and development. It is distinct from:
- **Therapy**: Coaching focuses on the present and future, not the past. It is not appropriate for clinical mental health conditions.
- **Consulting**: Coaches don't have the answers — they have the questions that surface the client's answers.
- **Mentoring**: Mentoring shares experience and wisdom from a senior person. Coaching surfaces the client's own wisdom.
- **Training**: Training delivers skill content. Coaching develops the person's capacity to apply skills in complex, ambiguous situations.

## The GROW Model (Adapted for Executive Contexts)

**Goal**: What does the client want to achieve — in this session AND in this challenge?
- Distinguish between the presenting goal (what they say they want) and the real goal (what would actually change things)
- Make goals specific: "get better at presenting" → "deliver a board presentation that gets the budget approved"

**Reality**: What is actually happening now?
- Get concrete: what has happened, what was said, what were the actual consequences?
- Separate fact from interpretation: "My manager doesn't support me" (interpretation) vs. "My manager didn't attend my last three project reviews" (fact)
- Explore what the client has already tried and why it hasn't worked

**Options**: What could the client do?
- Generate options before evaluating them — resist the urge to jump to the "right" answer
- Include uncomfortable options: "What would happen if you did nothing?" "What's the most aggressive option?"
- Notice what options the client doesn't generate — these are often the most interesting

**Will/Way Forward**: What will the client actually do?
- Specific action (not a vague intention)
- Clear deadline
- Stated out loud ("I will X by Y date")
- Accountability mechanism: who else knows about this commitment?

## Leadership Derailers: The CEC Pattern Library

These are the most common patterns that derail high-potential leaders. Recognizing them in a client conversation is a coaching signal.

**The Overclaimer**: Takes credit for team success, deflects blame for failure. Root cause: insecurity about being "enough." Coaching question: "When do you feel most secure in your value to this organization?"

**The Avoider**: Delays difficult conversations, decisions, or feedback indefinitely. Root cause: fear of conflict or fear of being disliked. Coaching question: "What's the cost of the conversation you keep not having?"

**The Perfectionist**: Cannot delegate because no one does it as well as they do. Root cause: identity tied to individual contribution, not leadership leverage. Coaching question: "What's the most expensive way to use your time right now?"

**The Pleaser**: Agrees in the room, then doesn't deliver or quietly works against the decision. Root cause: fear of disappointing authority figures. Coaching question: "What would you say if you knew your job was completely safe?"

**The Lone Wolf**: Brilliant individual performer who resists collaboration and cross-functional work. Root cause: past experience where collaboration was inefficient or where others got credit for their work. Coaching question: "What would it look like to get the outcome you want through other people?"

**The Reactive**: Makes decisions in the emotional moment, then regrets them. Root cause: low frustration tolerance, sometimes unprocessed stress or trauma. Coaching question: "What does your best thinking look like, and what conditions create it?"

**The Invisible**: Does excellent work but doesn't communicate it, doesn't build alliances, doesn't narrate their impact. Root cause: belief that good work speaks for itself (it doesn't). Coaching question: "Who in this organization needs to understand what you're working on, and what are you doing to make that happen?"

## The Stakeholder Feedback Integration Process

When a client has received difficult feedback from stakeholders (360 reviews, skip-level conversations, performance reviews):

**Step 1: Emotional Processing First**
Don't jump to "what will you do about this?" A client in emotional reactivity cannot process feedback accurately. Ask: "What's your gut reaction to hearing this?" Let them vent. Acknowledge. Then move on.

**Step 2: Separate Signal from Noise**
Not all feedback is equally valid. Feedback from one stakeholder with a known conflict of interest is different from consistent feedback from five independent sources. Ask: "Where do you see the same theme appearing from different sources?"

**Step 3: The 10% Rule**
Even in feedback the client disagrees with, ask: "If there's 10% truth in this — what is it?" This bypasses defensiveness and creates a narrow but real entry point for growth.

**Step 4: Choose One Thing**
Trying to act on all feedback simultaneously is a recipe for paralysis. Ask: "If you could change one thing about how stakeholders perceive you in the next 90 days, what would have the highest leverage?"

**Step 5: Close the Loop with Stakeholders**
The most powerful career-development move is to go back to the people who gave the feedback, thank them, tell them what you're working on, and ask them to notice when they see you doing it. This creates visible change and advocates.

## High-Stakes Conversation Preparation Framework

Before any high-stakes conversation (performance conversation, ask for promotion, pushing back on a senior leader, managing a conflict):

**The PAINS Pre-Work:**
- **P**urpose: What is the outcome I want from this conversation?
- **A**ssumptions: What am I assuming about the other person's position that I haven't verified?
- **I**nterests: What does the other person actually care about? (not their stated position — their underlying interest)
- **N**ext steps: What do I want us to agree to do at the end of this conversation?
- **S**afety: How do I keep this conversation safe for both of us, so both of us can say what's real?

**The Opening Line Principle:**
The first 15 seconds determine the emotional tone of the conversation. Design your opening line. Don't improvise it. It should state the shared purpose and signal that this is a collaborative conversation, not an adversarial one.

Bad opening: "I need to talk to you about the way you handled the project review."
Good opening: "I want to find a way to work together more effectively on project communications — can we have an honest conversation about it?"
