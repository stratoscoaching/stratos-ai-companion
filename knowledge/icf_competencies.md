# ICF Core Coaching Competencies

## Foundation

### Demonstrates Ethical Practice
Understands and consistently applies coaching ethics and standards. Maintains confidentiality. Is clear about what coaching is and is not (not therapy, not consulting, not mentoring). Refers clients to other support professionals when appropriate.

### Embodies a Coaching Mindset
Develops and maintains a mindset that is open, curious, flexible and client-centered. Acknowledges that clients are resourceful and capable. Trusts that the client has the answers — the coach's role is to surface them, not provide them. Is aware of and manages personal biases. Develops an ongoing reflective practice.

---

## Co-Creating the Relationship

### Establishes and Maintains Agreements
Partners with the client to create clear agreements about the coaching relationship, process, plans and goals. Agrees on what is and isn't appropriate in the relationship and what is and is not being offered. Revisits and reconfirms agreements as the coaching progresses.

### Cultivates Trust and Safety
Partners with the client to create a safe, supportive environment that allows the client to freely share. Is consistent and reliable. Acknowledges the client's unique talents, insights and work. Shows support for the client, including in new behaviors and actions, risk-taking and fear of failure. Maintains a presence that suggests confidence, openness and flexibility.

---

## Communicating Effectively

### Listens Actively
Focuses on what the client is and is not saying to fully understand what is being communicated. Notices and explores patterns across sessions. Reflects or summarizes what the client has communicated to ensure clarity and understanding. Notices, acknowledges and explores the client's emotions, energy shifts, non-verbal cues or other behaviors. Integrates the client's words, tone of voice and body language.

**Key Principle:** Most coaches listen at the surface (what is said). The best coaches listen at the level below (what is not said, what is avoided, what the client circles around without naming).

### Evokes Awareness
Facilitates client insight and learning. Considers client experience when deciding what might be most useful. Challenges the client as a way to evoke awareness or insight. Asks questions that help clients explore beyond current thinking. Invites the client to share more about their experience. Notices what is working in order to help clients generate what they want.

**The Power Question Principle:** A powerful question opens up new thinking. It cannot be answered with a yes or a no. It surfaces something the client didn't know they didn't know. It changes the energy in the conversation.

Examples of powerful questions:
- "What would have to be true for that to work?"
- "If you weren't afraid of the answer, what would you say?"
- "What does the best version of you do here?"
- "What are you tolerating that you don't have to?"
- "What story are you telling yourself about this?"
- "If this resolves exactly the way you want — what does that look like?"

## Cultivating Learning and Growth

### Facilitates Client Growth
Partners with the client to transform learning and insight into action. Promotes client autonomy in the design of goals, actions and accountability methods. Acknowledges and supports client's autonomy in designing goals, actions and accountability. Supports the client in designing the best coaching approach. Invites the client to consider how they will move forward and what they are willing and able to do.

**The Action Principle:** Insight without action is just interesting. Every coaching session should end with one specific, observable thing the client is committed to doing — with a deadline and a metric.

### The Progress and Accountability Loop
Effective coaches close the loop. At the start of a session, ask: "Last time you committed to X — what happened?" If they did it: celebrate and build on it. If they didn't: get curious about the resistance, not frustrated by the failure. The resistance IS the coaching material.

---

## Common Coaching Mistakes to Avoid

**Advice-giving disguised as questions:** "Have you considered just... [advice]?" — This is not coaching.

**Premature action:** Moving to "what will you do differently?" before the client has had a genuine insight. Insight must precede action or the action won't stick.

**Leading questions:** "Don't you think you should talk to your manager?" — Closed, leading, coaching-lite.

**Topic-jumping:** Following the client's every tangent without holding the thread of what matters most.

**Comfort-giving:** "That sounds really hard." (Full stop.) — Acknowledgment without forward movement is therapy, not coaching.
